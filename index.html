USER EXPERIENCE

What We Do

The goal of UX is to make applications more usable by better understanding the people who use them, their abilities as well as their limitations. We also focus on meeting specific business goals of any given application. We achieve these goals by creating informed design cultivated by research, analysis, cross-functional team collaboration and Lean UX.

MVP

MVP stands for Minimal Viable Product. It is a proceess that gives a new application just enough features to satisfy early adopters. Additional features are added based on feedback from the products additional use. This helps us get a usable product to our customers and meet our business goals more quickly as well as reduces the cost and recovery time from setbacks.

GOOB

GOOB stands for Get Out Of the Building. We are not the users; so any design decisions we make in meetings are only guesses. Far too often, the people most impacted by these the decisions we make have no part in the design process. For this reason, UX is dedicated to getting out of the building and to the users. This can be done with field obeservations, usability studies, users interviews and more. The decisons we make must be informed by the people using our software.

Collaboration

Collaboration among customers and small, cross-functional teams builds a shared understanding of the problem and aligns all parties on the best possible solution. This consensus on the decisions results in faster iterrations and increases buy-in from each individual team member as they have each contributed to the direction. In short, rather than focus on star performers, we rely on team cohesion and colloboration.

Journey Mapping

A journey map is a visualization of the process a person goes through to complete a particular task. It tracks not only the steps in a process but the user's emotions at any given step. This helps us to identify user pain points and gives us specific problems to solve within an application.

Ideation and Feasibility

Ideation refers to the generation of big ideas to solve an established problem by a cross funtional team . All ideas are mapped based on their impact to the customer and how feasible they are to build and maintain.

Of a Developer's Time is Spent on Rework

According to the Institutute of Electrical Engineers the US Government spent $60 billion on civillian and military IT project. According to Dr. Susan Weinschenk notes that up to 15% of those prjojects were abandoned and at least 50% of a programmers’ time during the project is spent doing avoidable rework.


The 1 - 10- 100 Rule

This concept developed by G. Loabovitz and Y. Chang illustrates the high cost of poor planning. This model states that requiremnts add to a procject increase by a factor of 10 at each phase, which means it's 10x more expensive to fix an issue in a prototype than it is in a deployed application


Reduced Development Time

A clear direction, early UX involvement and usability techniques have been shown to reduce development time by 33%-50% according to a 1998 study conducted by Forrester Research




Here are a few useful equations by Susaan Weinschenk to help quantify User Experience:


Error Costs

(# of errors) x (avg. repair time) x (employee cost) x (# of employees) = cost savings

Example: (2 errors/week) x (60 mins) x ($30/hour) x (100 employees) = $6,0000/week or $300,000/year



Development & Maintanence Costs

(# of changes) x (avg. hrs/change) x (cost of developer) x (4, if late) = cost savings



Productivity

(time saved) x (employee cost) x (# of employees) = cost savings

Example: (1 hr/week) x ($30/hr) x (1000 employees) = $30,000/ week or $15,000,000/year
